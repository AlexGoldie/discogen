Trajectory prediction is a branch of autonomous driving research focused on forecasting the future positions of traffic participants, such as vehicles, pedestrians, and cyclists, given their observed past motion and surrounding road context. Unlike simple extrapolation, effective trajectory prediction must account for the multi-modal nature of human behavior: at any moment, an agent may accelerate, brake, turn, or change lanes, leading to multiple plausible futures. The ability to anticipate these possibilities is critical for safe motion planning in self-driving systems.

Historically, trajectory prediction evolved from physics-based models that assumed constant velocity or acceleration, through classical machine learning approaches using hand-crafted features such as social forces and Gaussian processes. The field advanced significantly with the introduction of deep learning methods capable of jointly reasoning about agent interactions and scene context. Modern approaches typically encode the observed trajectories of multiple agents along with map information representing road geometry, lane boundaries, and traffic rules, then decode multiple possible future trajectories with associated probabilities.

The objective of trajectory prediction is to produce a set of plausible future trajectories for a target agent, each with an associated likelihood, that collectively cover the space of probable outcomes. Models are evaluated on how closely their best predictions match the ground truth trajectory, using metrics such as minimum Average Displacement Error (minADE), minimum Final Displacement Error (minFDE), miss rate, and Brier-minFDE. Success requires balancing prediction accuracy with diversity—covering multiple modes of behavior without diluting the quality of any single prediction.

In practice, trajectory prediction is a core component of autonomous driving stacks, where downstream planners rely on predicted trajectories to make safe and efficient driving decisions. It also finds applications in robotics, surveillance, sports analytics, and crowd simulation. Understanding agent dynamics, social interactions, and environmental constraints is essential before implementing a prediction model, as these factors fundamentally shape how agents move through the world.
